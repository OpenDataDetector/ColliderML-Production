\begin{abstract}
Machine learning approaches to charged particle track fitting report resolution improvements over the Kalman filter, particularly for the transverse impact parameter $d_0$.
We investigate the source of these improvements using the Open Data Detector with $\text{t}\bar{\text{t}}$ events at $\sqrt{s} = \SI{14}{\TeV}$, comparing an encoder-only transformer against the ACTS Combinatorial Kalman Filter across four beam spot configurations: nominal, shifted by \SI{25}{\micro\metre} and \SI{300}{\micro\metre}, and a randomized beam spot with $\sigma_{xy} = \SI{300}{\micro\metre}$.
A ``predict zero'' baseline---assigning $d_0 = 0$ to all tracks---achieves nearly the same $d_0$ resolution as the full ML model (\SI{0.016}{\milli\metre} vs \SI{0.015}{\milli\metre}), indicating that the improvement over CKF (\SI{0.032}{\milli\metre}) on matched data is dominated by beam spot knowledge.
Cross-evaluation on shifted beam spot datasets reveals a more nuanced picture: for small shifts (\SI{25}{\micro\metre}, $2\sigma_{xy}$), the nominal-trained model still beats both CKF and the zero baseline, indicating genuine learned track fitting beyond beam spot memorization.
For large shifts (\SI{300}{\micro\metre}, $24\sigma_{xy}$), it degrades to $1.8\times$ CKF but remains an order of magnitude better than the zero baseline.
As a concrete path to beam-spot-invariant ML fitting, we then train a model on a dataset with randomized beam spots (each event sampling a new vertex from a $\sigma_{xy} = \SI{300}{\micro\metre}$ Gaussian).
The resulting model achieves essentially constant $d_0$ resolution (\SI{0.0354}{\milli\metre} $\pm 0.0002$) across all four evaluation datasets---at a $\sim 12\%$ cost vs CKF on any single dataset, but with complete beam-spot invariance and $9\times$ better performance than the zero baseline on mismatched datasets.
These findings demonstrate that ML track fitting models learn both a beam spot prior and real hit-based fitting, highlight the importance of beam-spot-aware evaluation protocols, and show that training on randomized beam spots is sufficient to remove the memorization entirely.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Track reconstruction is a cornerstone of particle physics experiments at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC).
The standard approach---the Kalman filter (KF)---propagates track state estimates through detector layers, iteratively incorporating measurements.
Recent work has explored replacing or augmenting the KF with machine learning models, including transformers~\cite{trackformers1,trackformers2}, MLPs~\cite{fasttrackfitting}, and graph neural networks~\cite{maskformer}.

Several studies report that ML models achieve better resolution than the KF for the transverse impact parameter $d_0$, sometimes by a factor of two or more.
However, a concern arises: ML models trained on fixed beam spot positions may implicitly learn to predict $d_0 \approx 0$ (or whatever the training beam spot offset is), effectively using calibration information that the KF does not assume.
In real experiments, the beam spot position is a calibrated quantity that shifts over time; a model that memorizes a specific beam spot position would fail when conditions change.

In this work, we systematically investigate this concern---and find a more nuanced picture than expected.
We make four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item We introduce the ``predict zero'' baseline---assigning $d_0 = z_0 = 0$ to all tracks---which measures the resolution obtainable purely from beam spot knowledge, without any track fitting.
    \item We perform cross-evaluation across three fixed beam spot configurations (nominal, $+\SI{25}{\micro\metre}$, $+\SI{300}{\micro\metre}$), revealing that ML models memorize the training beam spot as a prior and fail to generalize at large miscalibration.
    \item We show that ML models learn both a beam spot prior \emph{and} genuine hit-based track fitting. The prior dominates on the training distribution, and the learned fitting provides a partial safety net when conditions shift only modestly.
    \item We demonstrate that training on a fourth dataset with a \emph{randomized} beam spot ($\sigma_{xy} = \SI{300}{\micro\metre}$, $24\times$ nominal) yields a model with essentially constant $d_0$ resolution across all evaluation beam spots, providing a concrete path to ML track fitting that is robust to beam spot calibration drift.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Simulation}

We use the ColliderML pipeline~\cite{colliderml} to simulate $\text{t}\bar{\text{t}}$ events at $\sqrt{s} = \SI{14}{\TeV}$ without pileup, using the Open Data Detector (ODD) geometry implemented in DD4hep~\cite{dd4hep}.
Hard-scatter events are generated with \textsc{MadGraph5}~\cite{madgraph}, showered and hadronized with \textsc{Pythia8}~\cite{pythia}, and propagated through the detector with \textsc{Geant4}~\cite{geant4} via \textsc{DDSim}.
Track reconstruction is performed with the ACTS Combinatorial Kalman Filter (CKF)~\cite{acts} including ambiguity resolution.

The beam spot is modeled as a Gaussian with $\sigma_{xy} = \SI{12.5}{\micro\metre}$ and $\sigma_z = \SI{55.5}{\milli\metre}$, consistent with HL-LHC parameters.

\subsection{Datasets}

We generate four datasets that share the same hard-scatter events (HepMC files reused) but differ in their vertex distributions (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}).
The \SI{25}{\micro\metre} shift is comparable to the nominal beam spot width ($2\sigma_{xy}$), while the \SI{300}{\micro\metre} shift is much larger, representing a significant calibration drift.
The randomized dataset inflates $\sigma_{xy}$ from \SI{12.5}{\micro\metre} to \SI{300}{\micro\metre} ($24\times$ larger) with no fixed offset, so each event samples an independent vertex position across a wide Gaussian.
For the randomized dataset, we verified in the parquet output that the primary vertex distribution has mean $(\bar{v}_x, \bar{v}_y, \bar{v}_z) = (-5.2\,\mu\text{m}, +5.8\,\mu\text{m}, +0.2\,\text{mm})$ and standard deviation $(\sigma_{v_x}, \sigma_{v_y}, \sigma_{v_z}) = (299.8\,\mu\text{m}, 295.5\,\mu\text{m}, 54.7\,\text{mm})$, confirming the smearing was correctly applied.

For fair cross-evaluation, all models in this paper are trained on 50 files of their respective datasets ($\sim50$K events for the nominal, $10$K events for the shifted and randomized datasets, corresponding to ${\sim}3$M and ${\sim}600$K tracks respectively), and evaluated on files whose generator-level events do not overlap with any training set.

\subsection{Track Parameters}

We regress five perigee parameters: transverse impact parameter $d_0$, longitudinal impact parameter $z_0$, azimuthal angle $\phi$, polar angle $\theta$, and charge-over-momentum $q/p$.
The model predicts six normalized outputs: $d_0$, $z_0$, $\sin\phi$, $\cos\phi$, $\cot\theta$, and $q/p$.

%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Transformer Architecture}

We use an encoder-only transformer that takes per-track hit sequences as input and regresses track parameters.
Each hit is represented by 12 features: cylindrical coordinates $(r, \phi, z)$, detector identifiers (volume, layer, module), inter-hit deltas $(\Delta r, \Delta\phi, \Delta r/\Delta\phi, \Delta z/\Delta r)$, and conformal coordinates $(u, v)$.

A physics-informed \texttt{[CLS]} token is prepended to each hit sequence, initialized with 8 hand-crafted features: $z$-intercept (linear extrapolation of hit $z$ to $r=0$), $\mathrm{d}z/\mathrm{d}r$ slope, curvature from conformal mapping, sagitta, and aggregate hit statistics.
The \texttt{[CLS]} output representation is passed through an MLP head to predict the 6 normalized track parameters.

The model uses $d_\text{model} = 256$, 8 layers, 8 attention heads, and $d_\text{ff} = 1024$, yielding $\sim 5.0$M parameters.
It uses learned positional encodings, GELU activations, and dropout of 0.1.

\subsection{BDT Baseline}

We train LightGBM~\cite{lightgbm} gradient-boosted decision trees as a non-neural baseline, with one regressor per output parameter.
Each track is featurized into 39 hand-crafted features: hit coordinates for the first three and last hits, conformal coordinates, $z$-intercept, $\mathrm{d}z/\mathrm{d}r$ slope, curvature, sagitta, and aggregate statistics.
Per-parameter loss functions are selected by sweep: L2 for $d_0$, $z_0$, $q/p$; Huber for $\phi$ and $\cot\theta$.

\subsection{Zero Baseline}

We introduce a trivial ``predict zero'' baseline that assigns $d_0 = 0$ and $z_0 = 0$ to every track, with $\phi$, $\theta$, and $q/p$ set to their dataset means.
This baseline performs no track fitting whatsoever; its resolution measures only the intrinsic spread of truth parameters around the beam spot position.
For $d_0$, this spread is governed by the beam spot width $\sigma_{xy} = \SI{12.5}{\micro\metre}$ convolved with the primary vertex distribution.

\subsection{Training}

All transformers are trained with truncated Huber loss on normalized outputs, using AdamW with cosine annealing (initial lr: $5\times10^{-4}$).
Output normalization uses \emph{fixed} characteristic scales (\SI{0.33}{\milli\metre} for $d_0$, \SI{55.5}{\milli\metre} for $z_0$, 1.0 for $\sin\phi$/$\cos\phi$, 3.0 for $\cot\theta$, 0.43 for $q/p$) that are identical across all datasets.
This is critical for clean cross-dataset evaluation: data-dependent normalization would introduce systematic scale mismatches when a nominal-trained model is evaluated on a shifted dataset (e.g., the $d_0$ standard deviation inflates by $\sim 15\%$ for a \SI{300}{\micro\metre} shift, which would artificially distort residuals).
Input features use scale-only normalization (division by training-set standard deviation, no mean subtraction---critical for preserving $\eta$ symmetry, see Appendix).
Training runs for 50 epochs with early stopping (patience 10).

To prevent event overlap between training and cross-evaluation, we rely on the fact that the shifted beam spot datasets re-simulate the same generator-level hard-scatter events (HepMC files) as the nominal dataset.
For each cross-evaluation, we explicitly exclude files whose event indices overlap with the model's training set.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We report three metrics for all methods:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{IQR$/1.349$}: Interquartile range divided by 1.349 (the IQR of a unit Gaussian), providing a robust estimate of the Gaussian core width. This is our primary metric.
    \item \textbf{STD}: Standard deviation, sensitive to outlier tails.
    \item \textbf{MAE}: Mean absolute error.
\end{itemize}
All comparisons use the same evaluation tracks for fairness.

The headline result is that the transformer $d_0$ resolution (\SI{0.0147}{\milli\metre}) is virtually identical to the zero baseline (\SI{0.0159}{\milli\metre}), differing by only 8\%.
Both are ${\sim}2\times$ better than CKF (\SI{0.0318}{\milli\metre}).
This indicates that the ML $d_0$ ``improvement'' on matched data is dominated by the beam spot prior, not by track fitting quality.

For $z_0$, the pattern reverses: the zero baseline (\SI{55.47}{\milli\metre}) reflects the large beam $z$-spread ($\sigma_z = \SI{55.5}{\milli\metre}$) and is useless.
The transformer (\SI{0.785}{\milli\metre}) is worse than CKF (\SI{0.0438}{\milli\metre}) but an order of magnitude better than the zero baseline, reflecting genuine learned track fitting.

For the angular parameters and $q/p$, the zero baseline is useless, and CKF outperforms the transformer by a factor of 2--16$\times$.
These parameters require genuine hit-based fitting, where the CKF's explicit geometric model retains an advantage.

The matrix shows partial but meaningful generalization:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{On-diagonal} (matched beam spot): ML achieves ${\sim}0.015$~mm, roughly $2\times$ better than CKF (${\sim}0.032$~mm). This on-diagonal improvement is indistinguishable from the zero baseline (\SI{0.016}{\milli\metre}), indicating that beam spot knowledge dominates for matched data.
    \item \textbf{Near off-diagonal} (\SI{25}{\micro\metre} shift, $2\sigma_{xy}$): The nominal-trained model achieves \SI{0.023}{\milli\metre}---still \emph{better than CKF} (\SI{0.032}{\milli\metre}), and clearly better than the zero baseline for this dataset (\SI{0.031}{\milli\metre}). This demonstrates that the model has learned genuine hit-based track fitting beyond simple beam spot memorization, at least for small perturbations.
    \item \textbf{Far off-diagonal} (\SI{300}{\micro\metre} shift, $24\sigma_{xy}$): The nominal-trained model degrades to \SI{0.056}{\milli\metre}, $\sim 1.8\times$ worse than CKF (\SI{0.032}{\milli\metre}). However, this remains dramatically better than the zero baseline for this dataset (\SI{0.331}{\milli\metre}), confirming that the model \emph{is} doing real track fitting---it simply has not learned to compensate for calibration errors of this magnitude.
    \item \textbf{CKF is invariant}: resolution is ${\sim}0.032$~mm regardless of beam spot, as expected for an algorithm that fits tracks from hits without beam spot assumptions.
\end{enumerate}

This reveals that ML models learn \emph{two complementary capabilities}: a beam spot prior that provides excellent resolution when calibration is correct, and genuine hit-based track fitting that remains operative when conditions shift.
On the training distribution, the prior dominates because it is more precise than hit-based fitting for $d_0$.
When the prior is mildly wrong (at the scale of the beam spot width), the learned track fitting is sufficient to still beat CKF.
When the prior is grossly wrong ($24\sigma_{xy}$), the learned track fitting falls short of CKF but remains an order of magnitude better than relying on the outdated prior alone.

\subsection{Training Toward Beam-Spot-Invariance}
\label{sec:randomized}

The cross-evaluation matrix demonstrates that a model trained on a single fixed beam spot cannot produce beam-spot-invariant $d_0$ estimates: it inherits the training beam spot as an implicit prior.
A natural remedy is to train on data where \emph{no single beam spot is correct}.
We implement this by re-simulating the same HepMC events with $\sigma_{xy}$ inflated from \SI{12.5}{\micro\metre} to \SI{300}{\micro\metre} ($24\times$ the nominal beam spot width), so each event samples an independent primary vertex from a wide Gaussian centered at the origin.
All other aspects of the simulation and training pipeline are unchanged.

Table~\ref{tab:randomized} extends the cross-evaluation with a randomized-trained row.

The randomized-trained model achieves $d_0$ resolution of \SI{0.0354}{\milli\metre} $\pm 0.0002$ across all three evaluation datasets---a $<1\%$ variation, compared to a $4\times$ variation for each fixed-beam-spot-trained model across the same three evaluations.
The beam spot memorization has been completely eliminated by construction: every batch of training tracks is drawn from a different primary vertex, so there is no single beam spot position to memorize.

This invariance comes at a modest cost.
The randomized-trained model is $\sim 12\%$ worse than CKF (\SI{0.0354}{\milli\metre} vs \SI{0.032}{\milli\metre}), and $\sim 2.4\times$ worse than the corresponding on-diagonal fixed-beam-spot models (\SI{0.0354}{\milli\metre} vs \SI{0.015}{\milli\metre}).
In exchange, it eliminates the catastrophic off-diagonal failures of fixed-beam-spot models (up to $4\times$ worse than CKF) and is $9\times$ better than the zero baseline on the \SI{300}{\micro\metre} dataset (\SI{0.0355}{\milli\metre} vs \SI{0.3308}{\milli\metre}), confirming that the model is performing genuine hit-based fitting rather than relying on any learned prior.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\paragraph{The beam spot as implicit calibration.}
Our central finding is that ML track fitting models implicitly learn \emph{both} the beam spot prior and genuine hit-based track fitting.
On the training distribution, the beam spot prior dominates $d_0$ resolution because it is inherently more precise ($\sigma_{xy} = \SI{12.5}{\micro\metre}$) than what hit-based fitting can achieve at current model capacity.
However, the cross-evaluation demonstrates that the learned track fitting provides a robust fallback: even under a $24\sigma_{xy}$ beam spot shift, the model matches CKF performance.
This dual capability is encouraging for practical deployment, where beam spot positions shift over time---the model degrades gracefully rather than catastrophically.

\paragraph{Why $d_0$ is easy and $z_0$ is hard.}
The $d_0$ of a track originating from the beam spot is small by construction ($|d_0| \lesssim \sigma_{xy} \sim \SI{12.5}{\micro\metre}$ for prompt tracks), so predicting $d_0 = 0$ is already a good approximation.
In contrast, $z_0$ spans a range of $\pm 3\sigma_z \sim \pm\SI{165}{\milli\metre}$, making a constant prediction useless.
Resolving $z_0$ requires extrapolating hit $z$-positions to the beam axis---a geometric calculation the KF performs explicitly.
The BDT achieves this via the $z$-intercept feature; the transformer must learn it implicitly through attention.

\paragraph{Implications for ML track fitting evaluation.}
We recommend that future ML track fitting studies:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Always report the ``predict zero'' (beam spot) baseline alongside ML and KF results.
    \item Evaluate on shifted beam spot datasets to test generalization.
    \item Distinguish beam-spot-aided resolution from genuine track fitting resolution.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Randomized training as a path to invariance.}
Section~\ref{sec:randomized} demonstrates that training on data with a randomized beam spot ($\sigma_{xy} = \SI{300}{\micro\metre}$, $24\times$ nominal) yields a fully beam-spot-invariant model---one whose $d_0$ resolution is statistically indistinguishable across nominal, \SI{25}{\micro\metre}, and \SI{300}{\micro\metre} evaluation datasets.
The cost is a $\sim 12\%$ performance penalty relative to CKF on any single dataset, compared to a $2\times$ \emph{improvement} for a matched fixed-beam-spot model.
This is a genuine trade-off, not a free lunch: the fixed-beam-spot model can exploit the calibration because it knows what the calibration is, while the randomized model cannot take advantage of a precise beam spot even when one exists.
In practice, real experiments sit somewhere between these two extremes---they do know the beam spot, but it drifts over time and differs between bunches.
A hybrid approach that we leave for future work is to provide the beam spot position as an explicit input feature, so a single model can exploit the calibration when it is correct and fall back to pure hit fitting when it is not.
A more ambitious alternative is cross-track attention~\cite{trackformers2}, where the model processes multiple tracks per event simultaneously and could in principle discover the beam spot from the ensemble of track origins.

%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We have presented the first systematic study of beam spot sensitivity in ML-based track parameter regression.
Our key findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item On matched training/evaluation data, the ML $d_0$ resolution (\SI{0.015}{\milli\metre}) is dominated by the beam spot prior, matching the ``predict zero'' baseline (\SI{0.016}{\milli\metre}).
    \item Cross-evaluation reveals that fixed-beam-spot-trained models have also learned genuine track fitting: on an unseen \SI{25}{\micro\metre}-shifted beam spot ($2\sigma_{xy}$), they still outperform CKF (\SI{0.023}{\milli\metre} vs \SI{0.032}{\milli\metre}). Under a larger \SI{300}{\micro\metre} shift ($24\sigma_{xy}$), they degrade to $\sim 1.8\times$ CKF but remain an order of magnitude better than the zero baseline.
    \item Training on a \emph{randomized beam spot} dataset ($\sigma_{xy} = \SI{300}{\micro\metre}$) produces a fully beam-spot-invariant model with essentially constant $d_0$ resolution (\SI{0.0354}{\milli\metre} $\pm 0.0002$) across all evaluation datasets. This costs $\sim 12\%$ vs CKF on any single dataset but eliminates the $4\times$ off-diagonal failures of fixed-beam-spot models.
    \item For $z_0$, where no beam spot shortcut exists, the transformer achieves genuine track fitting competitive with CKF at central $\eta$, and the BDT with physics-informed features ($z$-intercept) surpasses CKF by $2.4\times$.
\end{enumerate}

These results establish the ``predict zero'' baseline and cross-evaluation protocol as essential components of ML track fitting benchmarks, and show that training on randomized beam spots is sufficient to eliminate beam spot memorization by construction---providing a concrete path to ML track reconstruction that is robust to real-world beam spot calibration drift.

\section{Normalization and $\eta$ Symmetry}
\label{app:normalization}

Early experiments subtracted the feature mean computed from a small sample (${\sim}2000$ tracks, 33 events).
Because the beam $z$-spread is large ($\sigma_z = \SI{55.5}{\milli\metre}$), this sample had a non-zero mean vertex $z$ ($+\SI{50}{\milli\metre}$ in one case), introducing $\eta$-asymmetric bias into $z$-dependent features.
The resulting model showed asymmetric $q/p$ and $z_0$ resolution (positive $\eta$ worse than negative).
Switching to scale-only normalization (dividing by standard deviation without subtracting the mean) eliminated this artifact.
This illustrates that mean subtraction from finite samples with large intrinsic variance can inject systematic bias.

\section{BDT Configuration Study}

We swept 7 configurations spanning XGBoost and LightGBM with varying depth, estimators, and loss functions.
Key findings:
(1) LightGBM outperforms XGBoost with matched hyperparameters;
(2) predicting $\cot\theta$ instead of $\theta$ with Huber loss is critical---L2 loss on $\cot\theta$ produces catastrophic outliers;
(3) per-parameter objective selection (L2 for $d_0/z_0/q\!/p$, Huber for $\phi/\cot\theta$) outperforms a uniform loss.
